Braindead

written by Nathaniel Blackburn on 2022-02-12

I was drunk while I watched this so I'm not going to go into the serious detail that I had before. This is a horror comedy, so it was interesting to see how it did and did not take itself seriously.

The major part that I found interesting from this is the takeaway for what to do in a situation where zombies are real. The first and foremost thing is to work on yourself. Your personal failings may get you killed in such a situation. Whether that's not being able to let your mother go, head over heels for someone else, or being too caught up in greed to see reason, it can be destrimental to your survival to not be well adjusted. The next thing is carry with you any occult items that someone gives you that they state will keep you safe.

People that were evil when they were alive are even more dangerous when they're undead.

There were some pretty messed up parts. It was funny in the beginning when they kept loping pieces off the guy that found the rat monkey. The zombie baby scene where the main character kept punching it in public was funny. Also trying to put it in the blender.

Maybe looks are deceiving and not everyone that is physically attractive is good and not everyone god awfully ugly is also bad.

Big Trouble in Little China

written by Nathaniel Blackburn on 2022-02-09

Plot:

A white trucker wins a bet during a delivery to a chinatown, must follow the old friend he won the bet on a wild journey full of kung-fu, chinese black magic, guns, and human trafficking in order to collect.

Structure:

  • The main events that took place were very linear. The betting, going to pick up the girlfriend from the airport, the chase to try to save her, preparing to go in to Lo Pan's to get her, etc. It's very cut and dry without any fat to trim.

Characters:

  • Jack Burton, the bumbling white guy that is always trying to make a catch phrase. Literally no character development throughout the entire film, is continuously trying to be a macho guy, asking questions he doesn't seem willing or capable of hearing the answers to, and is continuously in over his head.
  • Gracie Law, troublesome attorney attempting to help ensure Asian immigrants are able to arrive safely and not get kidnapped in the process, seems to be causing more trouble than she's actually solving on her own. Becomes a liability almost immediately and only helped to bring someone else who was also a liability to the party.
  • Wang Chi, the friend of Jack that lost the original bet. Is trying to bring an old girl friend to the states from China. Is willing to face literal ancient evils for a girl and uses the fact that the white guy doesn't know Chinese culture to persuade him to help.

Diaglogue:

  • The dialogue was typical, largely one-sided characters giving announcements that move the plot along whenever there's a break between the fighting. They give just enough background in order to keep things moving along and let the viewer know where in the compound that they currently are and the troubles that they are facing.
  • There was a knowing sentiment that what was being said was a joke. It was relieving that it wasn't all serious and there were quite a few jokes strewn in.

Critique Methodology

written by Nathaniel Blackburn on 2022-02-07

Criteria

This is the criteria that will be used when critiquing all of the horror films. I have taken this info from this site. The main points focus around the plot, structure, characters, dialogues, and scenes of the film. I will use these points to grade individually and then come to an overall score. Another important aspect for these movies is whether they are good enough to watch again, so I will be adding that as a separate thing as even an arguably bad film may still be worth rewatching.

How to Critique a Film’s Plot

The plot is the flow of events and actions that consequently develop in a story. To discuss the film’s plot in your movie critique, you need to do the following:

  • Decide whether the plot is predictable.
  • Define whether some actions were unpredictable.
  • If they were, express your views considering an unexpected turn of events that shocked you.
  • See if the story corresponds to the characters’ motivations.

How to Critique a Film’s Structure

The movie’s structure is how the parts relate to each other or how the plot is built. Remember that the structure encompasses the following parts:

  • Exposition (introducing the key characters and their circumstances)
  • Rising action (mounting tension that leads to the story’s climax)
  • Climax
  • Falling action
  • Denouement (a resolution that brings the story to an end)

How to Critique the Movie’s Characters

Characterization is the description of the characters’ personalities, beliefs, motivations, etc. To discuss the film’s characterization in your movie critique, you need to do the following:

  • Concentrate on specific movie characters.
  • Set your priorities: some movie characters do not need any serious analysis.
  • See if the characters’ dress, talk, act, or look corresponds with the impression they should create.
  • Remember that the better the characters are developed, the more character-driven the story is.
  • Consider that the right motivation of the characters makes the audience believe the story.
  • Remember that good dialogues should not be protracted as they are to sound natural.
  • See if there is a logical development of the conversation.
  • Watch the body language of the actors and decide whether their gestures correspond to their words or not.

How to Criticize the Movie’s Scenes

A scene is a self-contained episode within a larger work. To discuss the scenes in your movie critique, you should do the following:

  • Determine whether the scenes were well-played by the actors and well-shot by the operator.
  • Mind that they are to develop smoothly as a part of a larger dramatic unit.
  • Remember that scenes should build a chain that makes up the story.
  • Examine if they contain some conflict and how the characters typically react to it.
  • Try to follow through each scene to make sure that it properly transitioned into the next one.
